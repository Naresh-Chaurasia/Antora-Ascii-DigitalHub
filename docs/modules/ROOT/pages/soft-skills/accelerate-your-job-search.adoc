=== Identify your transferable skills

*What Are Transferable Skills?*

Transferable skills are abilities you develop in one situation that can be applied to a completely different context, such as a new job. These skills come from all kinds of life experiences, not just paid work.

*Why Transferable Skills Matter*

* You already have valuable skills from your life experiences
* They help you decide which jobs to pursue
* They help you explain your background on a resume
* They help you confidently talk about yourself in interviews

Many employers now use *skills-based hiring*, meaning they focus more on relevant skills than on formal education or previous job titles.

*Examples of Transferable Skills*

Transferable skills can be both technical and non-technical.

*Technical Skills*

* Programming
* Data analysis
* Technical troubleshooting

*Non-Technical (Soft) Skills*

* #Communication#
* Adaptability
* #Problem-solving#
* Teamwork
* Customer service
* Leadership

These skills are often just as important to employers as technical skills.

*Why Transferable Skills Are Hard to Identify*

* Your skills feel natural or “second nature” to you
* You may underestimate experiences outside of formal jobs
* You may not realize employers value these skills

*How to Identify Your Transferable Skills*

*Step 1: Reflect on Your Life Experiences*

Think beyond job titles and paid work.
Consider:

* Volunteering
* #Mentoring#
* Coaching sports
* Leadership roles
* School activities
* Community involvement

*Step 2: Identify Skills Gained from Each Experience*

Ask yourself:

* Did I handle administrative or detail-oriented tasks?
* Did I plan events or manage schedules?
* Did I communicate with different groups?
* Did I brainstorm ideas or solve problems?

Write down the skills you developed in each situation.

*Step 3: Connect Skills to Potential Jobs*

Think about how your skills apply to other roles.

Examples:

* Executive Assistant → Project Manager  
  - Scheduling, coordination, communication, planning

* Team-based experiences (sports, military, school) → Many roles  
  - Collaboration, teamwork, adaptability, shared goal achievement

*Key Takeaway*

You already have more job-ready skills than you might think. By recognizing and clearly communicating your transferable skills, you can strengthen your resume, perform better in interviews, and expand your career opportunities.

*Conclusion*

Transferable skills come from real life. When you identify them and align them with job requirements, you position yourself strongly for new roles—even in fields you haven’t worked in before.

=== Mike: Identify your most valuable transferable skills

*Background*

Mike is a Product Lead at the Google School for Leaders, where he leads teams building learning experiences for managers and leaders across Google. *He previously worked as a teacher*, and the skills he developed in the classroom now directly apply to his role as a product lead.

*What Are Transferable Skills?*

Transferable *skills are abilities learned in one environment that can be applied across different roles, industries, and careers*.

*Teaching Skills Applied to Product Leadership*

Skills developed in the classroom that translate to leadership roles include:

* Prioritization
* Time management
* Navigating uncertainty
* Adaptability
* Communication with senior leaders
* Collaboration with colleagues

*Four Key Transferable Skills Employers Value*

*Communication*

* Ability to tailor messages to different audiences
* Clear and concise delivery
* Adapting communication style based on who you’re speaking to

*Problem-Solving*

* Ability to respond effectively when challenges arise
* Demonstrates critical thinking and decision-making
* Shows a holistic approach to situations

*Navigating Ambiguity*

* Comfort with change and uncertainty
* Ability to respond effectively to shifting circumstances
* Maintaining a positive impact on others during change

*Emotional Regulation*

* Managing emotions during stressful or uncertain situations
* Showing up consistently for the team
* Demonstrating self-awareness and emotional intelligence

*Why These Skills Matter*

* Employers value skills that transfer across roles and industries
* These skills show how you think, adapt, and collaborate
* Strong transferable skills help teams grow and perform at a higher level

*Key Takeaway*

Your past experiences—no matter the role—have equipped you with valuable, transferable skills. When you can clearly explain how you use communication, problem-solving, adaptability, and emotional regulation, you present a more complete and compelling picture to employers.

=== Understand Career Identity Statement
 
* Career Identity Statement = Your professional "North Star" guiding job search and networking.
* Purpose:
** *Provides a quick, insightful overview of your skills, interests, goals, and passions*.
** Goes beyond listing qualifications; shows what makes you unique.
* Example:
** "My background in finance and customer service has equipped me with strong analytical skills and a client-focused approach. I excel at using data to solve problems effectively, and I'm committed to clear communication and fostering positive interactions. I highly value teamwork, and my passion for basketball continually reinforces the importance of collaboration and communication in achieving shared goals."
* Why it works:
** Highlights a unique mix of skills and experiences.
** Combines analytical ability with customer focus.
** Adds personal touch through passions.
* Tips for creating your own:
** Avoid vague phrases; use specific skills and examples.
** Keep it focused on key aspects of your professional self.
** Maintain a positive, genuine tone.
** Aim for clarity and conciseness.
* Where to use:
** Networking introductions.
** Cover letters or job inquiry emails.
** Professional bios and online profiles.
* Benefits:
** Helps present your authentic self.
** Narrows job search to roles matching your goals and values.

=== Understand resume writing best practices

*Tailoring Your Resume – Summary*

*Why Tailoring Your Resume Matters*

A resume is often an employer’s first impression of you and acts as a marketing tool. A one-size-fits-all resume is ineffective because different jobs—even with the same title—can require different skills.

*Recruiters are more likely to hire candidates who tailor their resumes to each role.* In fact, many recruiters spend only a few seconds reviewing each resume, so relevance and clarity are critical.

*Key Insight*:
83% of recruiters are more likely to hire candidates who *tailor their resume to a specific job*.

*Same Job Title, Different Skill Needs*:
Even identical job titles can have different expectations.

Example:

* UX Designer at a large e-commerce company  
  - Extensive user research  
  - Designing for complex websites  

* UX Designer at a startup  
  - Rapid prototyping  
  - A/B testing for mobile apps  

This makes tailoring your resume essential for every application.

*Three Key Areas to Focus On*

*1. Resume Content*

*Show Measurable Results*

* Use numbers and data to demonstrate impact
* Example:
  - Instead of: “Increased sales”
  - Use: “*Increased new product sales by 30% versus* quota by closing eight new accounts in under three months”

*Translate Experience into Transferable Skills*

* Focus on skills gained, not just responsibilities
* Example:
  - Store clerk experience → communication and customer service
  - *Resulted in a 20% increase in positive customer reviews*

*Use Impactful Language*

* Use strong action verbs and precise wording
* Emphasize your active role and ownership

Example:

* Before: “Made a new company dashboard”
* After: “Designed and built a real-time interactive dashboard enabling faster decision-making for five partner teams”

*Add Specific Context*

* Avoid vague statements
* Provide concrete examples and proof
* Example:
  - *Mentored junior team members and helped troubleshoot complex technical issues*

*2. Resume Structure*

*Keep It Concise*

* Ideally one page
* Maximum two pages
* Shows prioritization and clarity

*Make Skills Easy to Find*

* Highlight key skills prominently
* Use bullet points
* *Include keywords from the job description*

*Ensure Logical and Scannable Layout*

* Use section titles, bullets, spacing, and emphasis
* *Avoid large blocks of text*
* Make it easy for recruiters to scan quickly

*3. Resume Personalization*

*Use Job Description Keywords*

* Identify important words and phrases
* Incorporate them naturally into your resume

*Align with Job Responsibilities*

* Match your bullet points to the role’s requirements
* Show clear connections between your experience and the job

*Be Selective*

* Do not list everything you’ve ever done
* Emphasize relevant experiences
* Remove or shorten less relevant details

*Key Takeaway*

A strong resume is concise, tailored, and results-driven. By aligning your content, structure, and language with each job description, you make it easy for recruiters to quickly see why you’re a strong match.

*Conclusion*

Tailoring your resume takes effort, but it significantly increases your chances of standing out. Focus on relevance, clarity, and impact—and keep it brief.
